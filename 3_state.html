Why the State Rises

Why Successful Societies Fall—and How to Prevent It

If stateless societies worked so well, why don't they exist everywhere today? This is a crucial question. Understanding why historically successful anarchist societies eventually fell to state power—and why popular narratives about freedom mislead us—is essential for building something that lasts.

This page examines both the historical patterns that led to the conquest of free societies and the modern myths that prevent us from learning those lessons. The goal isn't pessimism—it's preparation. By understanding how freedom has been lost in the past, we can design systems that resist those failure modes in the future.

How Stateless Societies Fell

The historical examples we examined—medieval Iceland, Somali Xeer, Irish Brehon Law, and others—didn't collapse from internal dysfunction. They were conquered by external state powers or corrupted from within by centralizing forces. Understanding these patterns is crucial.

Pattern 1: External Conquest

Most successful stateless societies were eventually conquered by neighboring states with centralized military force. This wasn't because decentralized defense doesn't work—it's because states dedicate enormous resources to conquest that decentralized societies don't match.

The Conquest Pattern:

  • Iceland (1262): Fell not to direct military conquest but to internal factionalism exploited by the Norwegian crown. Powerful families sought external backing in disputes, inviting state power into a stateless system.
  • Ireland (1600s): Brehon Law was systematically suppressed by English conquest and colonization. The British imposed common law by force, making Brehon practice illegal.
  • Neutral Moresnet (1919): Dissolved by treaty after World War I, absorbed into Belgium despite functioning peacefully for over a century.

The lesson: stateless societies can defend themselves effectively, but they're vulnerable when surrounded by aggressive state powers, especially when internal factions invite state intervention to settle disputes.

Pattern 2: Internal Centralization

Some societies transitioned from stateless to stateful gradually, as certain individuals or families accumulated power and created state-like structures. This process is subtle and often seems necessary or beneficial at first.

Warning Signs of Centralization:

  • Hereditary authority: When leadership becomes hereditary rather than voluntary, power begins concentrating
  • Monopolization of justice: When one arbitration system becomes dominant and others are suppressed
  • Taxation in all but name: When "contributions" become mandatory or enforced through social pressure
  • Standing forces: When defensive militias become permanent armies controlled by elites
  • Legal privileges: When certain individuals gain exemptions from customary law

The lesson: eternal vigilance is required. Stateless societies must actively resist centralization, not assume decentralization will maintain itself.

Pattern 3: Integration and Assimilation

Sometimes stateless societies were simply absorbed into larger state systems through economic integration, cultural pressure, or gradual adoption of state norms. This is the slow death—a stateless society that voluntarily adopts state structures because everyone around them has states.

The lesson: strong cultural identity and philosophical commitment to voluntaryism is essential. Without ideological grounding, people drift toward statism because it's culturally dominant.

Why Movies Mislead Us

Popular culture—especially Hollywood—teaches terrible lessons about how freedom is achieved and maintained. These narratives are worse than useless; they actively harm liberation movements by promoting strategies that fail.

The Hero Narrative

Nearly every movie about revolution or freedom follows the same formula: a charismatic leader rallies followers, confronts the tyrant in a climactic battle, wins, and everyone lives happily ever after. This narrative is fiction—and dangerous fiction at that.

Why the Hero Narrative Fails:

  • Creates dependency on leaders: People wait for a hero instead of taking responsibility themselves
  • Ignores systemic change: Defeating one tyrant doesn't dismantle tyrannical systems
  • Promotes violent confrontation: Direct conflict with states typically strengthens them (emergency powers, rallying public support)
  • Oversimplifies solutions: Freedom requires building alternative systems, not just destroying the current one
  • Ignores economics: Revolution movies rarely show how people eat, trade, or organize production

Examples: Braveheart, The Hunger Games, V for Vendetta, Star Wars—all feature heroic confrontation leading to victory. In reality, violent rebellion against states usually results in either failure or replacement of one state with another (often worse).

The "Capture the State" Narrative

Another common story: good people infiltrate the government, gain positions of power, and reform the system from within. This strategy has a 100% historical failure rate for producing lasting freedom.

Why This Strategy Fails:

  • The state corrupts reformers: You don't change the system; it changes you
  • Power structures preserve themselves: Real threats to state power are neutralized before they succeed
  • Compromise becomes betrayal: Working within the system requires accepting its fundamental premises
  • Success creates new tyrants: Even successful "reformers" rarely relinquish power once obtained

Every "change from within" story ends either with the reformer becoming corrupt or being destroyed by the system. The state is not a neutral tool—it's a mechanism of control that resists being used for liberation.

The "Vote Harder" Narrative

Countless movies and shows promote voting as the path to change. Get the right people elected, pass the right laws, and freedom follows. This ignores that voting is the process by which majorities impose their will on minorities through state violence.

Even if you elect "good" representatives, you're still legitimizing the system that grants some people authority over others. You're still participating in the mechanism that makes your preferences subject to override by others. Voting cannot produce voluntary society—it can only produce different flavors of coercion.

The "Violent Revolution Then Democracy" Narrative

Perhaps the most dangerous narrative: violent overthrow of tyranny followed by establishment of democratic government. This pattern—common in historical dramas—consistently produces new tyrannies in reality.

Historical Reality:

  • French Revolution: Terror, then Napoleon, then monarchy again
  • Russian Revolution: Bolsheviks replaced Tsar with worse tyranny
  • Chinese Revolution: Mao's regime killed tens of millions
  • Cuban Revolution: Castro's dictatorship continues decades later

The pattern is clear: violent revolution creates power vacuums that attract the most ruthless competitors. The victor isn't the noblest—it's whoever is willing to use maximum violence. Then they use the apparatus their revolution created to impose their own vision.

What Actually Works

If heroic confrontation, political reform, and violent revolution don't work, what does? History and logic point to a different approach: building the alternative while peacefully withdrawing from the existing system.

Agorism: Counter-Economics in Practice

Instead of confronting the state, starve it. Instead of reforming it, make it irrelevant. Build parallel structures that provide what people need without state involvement:

  • Economic secession: Trade outside the system (black markets, grey markets, cryptocurrency)
  • Alternative institutions: Build schools, arbitration, security, insurance without state involvement
  • Technological circumvention: Use tools that make state control impossible (encryption, decentralized networks)
  • Cultural shift: Change minds about legitimacy, not just policy preferences

This is what the Network does. We're not fighting the state—we're building the alternative and inviting people to join it. When enough people participate in the parallel economy, the state becomes irrelevant. See Why Agorism Works for detailed strategy.

Decentralization and Redundancy

The historical examples show that centralization is the path to failure. The solution is building robust, decentralized networks with no single point of failure:

  • No leaders who can be co-opted or eliminated
  • No central location that can be raided or destroyed
  • No hierarchical structure that can be infiltrated from the top
  • Multiple overlapping systems for every essential service

This is why the Network emphasizes distributed organization. See Network Structure for how this works in practice.

Ideological Commitment

Perhaps most importantly: successful voluntary societies require strong philosophical grounding. People need to understand why statelessness matters, not just how it works. Without that understanding, they'll recreate state structures at the first crisis.

This is why we emphasize philosophy alongside practical action. Read Intro to Voluntaryism and Voluntaryism Plus to understand the principles that make freedom sustainable.

Defensive Preparation

History shows that external threats remain real. Peaceful societies must be prepared to defend themselves effectively without becoming militaristic. This means:

  • Decentralized defense capabilities (no single military to capture)
  • Economic resilience (can't be starved into submission)
  • Cultural cohesion (shared values that resist divide-and-conquer)
  • Strategic deterrence (making conquest too costly to attempt)

See Collective Defense for how stateless societies can defend themselves effectively.

Learning From the Past, Building for the Future

The downfall of historical stateless societies teaches crucial lessons:

  1. External threats are real: Build defensive capability without creating centralized military power
  2. Internal factionalism invites tyranny: Develop robust conflict resolution that doesn't require outside authority
  3. Centralization creeps in slowly: Actively resist it rather than assuming freedom maintains itself
  4. Cultural isolation is dangerous: Build broad networks rather than isolated communes
  5. Philosophical grounding matters: People need to understand principles, not just follow practices

The misleading narratives in popular culture teach us to:

  1. Reject hero worship: Build distributed movements, not cults of personality
  2. Reject political strategies: Don't try to capture or reform state power
  3. Reject violent revolution: Build alternatives, don't destroy and hope for the best
  4. Focus on economics: Trade, production, and exchange are how freedom spreads
  5. Play the long game: Lasting change takes time and sustained effort

The Network incorporates these lessons. We're building distributed, resilient structures based on voluntary exchange and strong philosophical principles. We're not waiting for heroes or hoping for political reform. We're doing the hard work of creating functional alternatives to state systems—alternatives that can persist and spread precisely because they avoid the failure modes of the past.
