The Intentional Partnership

Step Four: Knowing What You Want


Everyone has a need for security, relaxation, comfort and fun. If you don't get these needs met, you can run into problems with physical and mental health, and create coping patterns that damage your relationships.

Additionally, you deserve to have a partner who is excited about you.

The way the current world is organized makes it difficult to live in a way that gets all of these needs met.

A healthy relationship acknowledges that neither partner is at fault for these worldly difficulties. The current system is hard to navigate, especially while maintaining a lived experience of joy and security.

It is important to make sure that you know what you desire regardless of how feasible it is. The current culture tells us that we should only desire what is reasonable, but that is not the way our hearts work. We want the best possible world for ourselves, and if we deny this fact, we deny a part of our selves.

Activity:

Write out what is the best possible reality you could live in. Detail your ideal location, social setting, daily life, and what interactions would be like with your partner.

Next, list 5 activities that would bring you joy, regardless of whether they are productive in other ways.

In today's culture, there are many ideas that are proliferated as enduring truths but which are not actually true and which are instead damaging to relationships.

Some of these ideas include:

Culture Says:

  • You should not spend all your time with your partner.

    Reasoning: You would become suffocated. No one would be able to do that because the passion would go away. Everyone gets boring or annoying eventually.

Our Answer:

It does not have to get uncomfortable to spend all of your time with someone. Your partner should excite you. You should want to spend all your time with them because you respect them and their opinion enough that you want to share all your experiences with them. Your life should be filled with shared meaning and exploration, such that any time away from each other would be missing out.

It is okay not to achieve this, but you should desire to. It might not be logistically possible, but you should value your shared time together and keep trying to get as much time as possible.

What about activities that your partner isn’t into or doesn’t want to do with you?

You should see the value of spending that time with your partner because of the positivity you get from being together. If they value something you think is unimportant or negative, then you or they might want to rethink those activities. The goal is to value each other's world view and to learn about what function each activity plays in your partner's life.

Culture Says:

  • You should have your own life path and passions separate from those of your partner.

    Reasoning: It's "healthy" to have your own passions so that you can differentiate yourself as a person separate from your partner.

Our Answer:

If you can find someone who has a passion for the same things that you do, then you have a wonderful base for a relationship. You can work with eachother, learn with each other, network with eachother and help each other through whatever problems may arise without having to juggle two separate vocations.

On the other hand, if you have your own separate passions, there will always be the possibility of someone out there being a better match for your partner, because that person is passionate about more of the same things. You want to give your partner the highest possible experience, which could include a partner who shares these passions.

Culture Says:

  • Humans are not meant to be in monogamous, long term relationships.

    Reasoning: Apes do not get married. Some tribes may not follow a monogamous structure.

Our Answer:

Being with your perfect partner forever is the best option if they are the right person.

A lifetime is not enough time to spend with someone who is your perfect life partner.

If there are no people in the world that you recognize as perfect for you, you just need to keep looking. Do not subject someone to a relationship with you when they could be finding their own perfect partner, even if they say they have given up. Perhaps their perfect partner is going to surprise them! Or yours might surprise you!

Society Says:

  • No one person will be able to meet all of your needs.

    Reasoning: It is "unhealthy" for you to expect one person to meet all of your needs because you have so many needs and that would overload the other person.

Our Answer:

You do not need to get any of your needs met outside of the relationship - but you are welcome to when the interaction is healthy. It is unhealthy to interact in a way that places another person in a position to grow closer to you than your partner.

If you get several of your needs met through friends and family, that is great. But the more needs you meet with your partner, the less likely you are to grow apart from eachother. There will be more understanding. What if you were stuck in a bunker with this person for several months?

We know from experience, it is possible!
