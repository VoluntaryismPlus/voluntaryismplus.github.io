Navigating Groups & Networks

Building Trust in Voluntary Associations

Navigating new groups and communities requires a thoughtful approach, one rooted in mutual respect and genuine connection. Our aim isn't to "join" or "prove ourselves" to any existing group, but rather to foster an environment where voluntary interactions thrive and individuals are empowered. This guide outlines principles for engaging with groups, ensuring that every connection is built on shared values and reciprocal benefit.

The Importance of Networking

We don't need to change people; we just want to give them another option—so they can do the thing that they would have done either way. There will always be objects and services to import and export. The question is: will these exchanges happen through coercive institutions, or through voluntary networks?

Every person you connect with authentically is a node in the network of freedom. Every group you engage with skillfully is a potential ally. The goal isn't recruitment—it's relationship building that creates space for voluntary cooperation.

Group Engagement Guide

When approaching a new association, prioritize building personal respect first. Let your actions speak to your character and competence. As you establish rapport and demonstrate your unique contributions, people will naturally become interested in what drives you. It's at this point that openly sharing your alignment with voluntaryist principles can be most effective, as they will perceive it as an authentic part of your valuable identity.

Phase 1: Observation & Understanding

Always be genuinely curious about the group's purpose and dynamics.

Before saying anything about yourself or your goals, invest time in understanding:

  • What is their stated purpose? Read their mission statement, charter, or founding documents if available
  • What is their actual purpose? What do they spend their time and resources on? Often different from what they say
  • Who makes decisions? Is power centralized or distributed? Formal or informal?
  • What are the unspoken rules? Every group has norms not written down
  • What do members value? Status? Competence? Ideology? Relationships?
  • What problems are they trying to solve? Understanding their pain points shows where you might add value

Ask open-ended questions to understand their intentions and goals. Listen more than you speak. Most people aren't used to genuine curiosity—it builds rapport quickly.

Phase 2: Value Demonstration

Show, don't tell

Actions speak louder than ideology. Before you talk about voluntaryism, anarchism, or any philosophical framework:

  • Contribute something useful - Solve a problem they have, fill a gap in their capabilities
  • Keep your commitments - If you say you'll do something, do it reliably and well
  • Respect their existing culture - Don't immediately try to change everything
  • Be helpful without being pushy - Offer assistance, don't force it
  • Demonstrate competence - Whatever your skills are, show them through action

Remember: every individual is a free agent. Just as you are evaluating the group, they are also assessing whether your presence adds value. It's a two-way relationship: the group gains something from your engagement, just as you seek benefit from theirs.

Phase 3: Transparent Alignment

Share your vision when they're curious

Once you've established credibility through contribution, people will naturally wonder what motivates you. This is when you articulate your own relationship to their concepts within your broader pursuit of freedom, transparently sharing any concerns you might have.

Good approach: "I'm interested in [their goal] because I believe voluntary cooperation is more effective than coercion. I've been learning about how decentralized networks can achieve [benefit] without relying on authority structures."

Bad approach: "You're all doing it wrong. Let me tell you about anarcho-capitalism..."

Ensure members feel a strong sense of identification with their group's stated purpose or name before you associate them with any label. Confirm their authentic alignment. Don't assume someone is an anarchist just because they criticize government—they might want different government, not no government.

Phase 4: Deep Integration or Graceful Exit

Decide if this is your group

After you've contributed value and shared your vision, assess:

  • Is there genuine value alignment? Not perfect agreement, but shared core principles
  • Is the relationship reciprocal? Are you giving and receiving value?
  • Does this serve the broader network? Will engagement here strengthen the cause of freedom?
  • Is your time well-spent here? Opportunity cost is real

If yes to most of these: deepen your involvement. Take on more responsibility. Build stronger relationships. Become a bridge between this group and the broader network.

If no to most of these: exit gracefully. Thank them for the experience. Leave the door open for future collaboration if circumstances change. Don't burn bridges unnecessarily.

Understanding Group Dynamics

Formal vs Informal Power

Every group has two power structures:

Formal power: The official hierarchy, stated decision-making process, written rules

Informal power: Who actually influences decisions, who people listen to, unwritten rules

Often these don't match. The stated leader may be a figurehead while real decisions happen in informal conversations. Understanding both structures is essential for effective engagement.

Free Agents, Not Cogs

Remember that every individual is a free agent. Approach interactions with the understanding that members are not simply cogs in a machine; they are unique contributors with their own goals, values, and agency.

This means:

  • Build relationships with individuals, not just "the group"
  • Recognize that people's alignment with group goals varies
  • Some members are more open to voluntaryist ideas than others
  • People can change groups; groups don't own people

The Two-Way Value Test

Never assume you're "applying" for a group; instead, recognize that the most successful groups are those that actively seek out and value your unique contributions.

Both sides should be asking:

  • You evaluating them: Does this group help me achieve my goals? Do I respect the people? Is my time well-spent here?
  • Them evaluating you: Does this person add value? Are they reliable? Do they fit our culture?

If an imbalance arises where a member consistently fails to contribute, it often signals a mismatch, and it may be more beneficial for them to redirect their energy toward endeavors they are truly passionate about.

Creating Internal Culture

Cultivate an internal culture within your own immediate network that is recognizable and easily understood by others through your consistent actions and clear communication.

What This Means:

  • Consistency: Your actions match your stated values
  • Reliability: You keep commitments
  • Clarity: People know what you stand for
  • Openness: You're willing to explain your approach
  • Results: Your methods produce visible benefits

When your internal culture is strong and attractive, you don't have to "recruit"—people naturally gravitate toward what works. They see your network functioning smoothly, solving problems effectively, and supporting members genuinely. They want in.

Red Flags: When to Walk Away

Not every group is worth engaging with. Some warning signs:

Immediate Disqualifiers:

  • Violates NAP: The group's core activities involve aggression or support for coercive systems
  • Cult dynamics: Members can't leave without punishment, leader is beyond criticism, thinking is discouraged
  • Fundamental dishonesty: The group regularly lies to outsiders or members
  • Parasitic: The group survives by extracting from others without creating value

Serious Concerns:

  • One-way extraction: You're expected to give constantly without receiving
  • Drama culture: Constant interpersonal conflicts, gossip, backstabbing
  • Resistance to improvement: Any suggestion for positive change is met with hostility
  • Mission drift: The stated purpose is just a front; something else is really happening
  • Entitlement: Members expect your time/resources as if they're owed it

Trust your gut. If something feels wrong, investigate. If it continues feeling wrong, leave. Your time and energy are precious—don't waste them on toxic groups.

Bridge Building vs Takeover

Wrong Approach: Takeover

Some activists try to "infiltrate and take over" existing groups. This usually fails because:

  • It's dishonest and people sense it
  • It creates justified resentment
  • It wastes energy on internal politics
  • Even if successful, you've inherited dysfunction

Right Approach: Bridge Building

Instead, become a bridge between the group and the broader network of freedom:

  • Help them achieve their stated goals using voluntaryist methods
  • Introduce them to resources and people in the network
  • Model effective decentralized coordination
  • Share what works without demanding they adopt your framework
  • Let results speak for themselves

When groups see that voluntary methods work better than coercive ones, they adopt them naturally. You don't need to take over—you just need to demonstrate superiority.

Networking Across Ideological Lines

You don't need someone to be a full anarchist to work with them productively. Many people who wouldn't identify as voluntaryists still:

  • Value freedom and autonomy
  • Distrust centralized power
  • Prefer voluntary solutions when practical
  • Want to help others directly
  • Recognize problems with the current system

These are natural allies on specific issues. You might collaborate with:

  • Libertarians on reducing regulation and taxation
  • Leftists on mutual aid and police accountability
  • Conservatives on gun rights and homeschooling freedom
  • Environmentalists on alternatives to industrial agriculture
  • Tech people on encryption and decentralization

The goal isn't to get everyone to identify as anarchists. The goal is to expand the scope of voluntary cooperation and reduce the scope of coercion. Anyone moving in that direction is an ally on that specific issue, even if you disagree on other things.

Multiple Membership Strategy

Don't put all your eggs in one basket. Engage with multiple groups simultaneously:

  • Core network: Your primary voluntaryist community
  • Professional groups: Related to your trade or skills
  • Local groups: Geographic community connections
  • Interest groups: Hobbies, learning, shared interests
  • Activist groups: Working on specific issues you care about

This creates:

  • Redundancy: If one group fails, you have others
  • Diverse perspectives: Avoid echo chambers
  • Broader reach: You become a connector between different networks
  • More opportunities: Different groups open different doors

But be strategic—don't spread yourself so thin that you're ineffective everywhere.

When Your Presence Doesn't Add Value

Sometimes you'll realize you're not a good fit for a group. This happens when:

  • Your skills aren't relevant to their needs
  • You can't contribute time consistently enough
  • Your values conflict too much with theirs
  • You're not interested in their actual work (only their stated mission)
  • The group is already overstaffed in your area

This isn't failure—it's wisdom. Redirect your energy where you can have real impact. It's better to contribute meaningfully to fewer groups than to be a marginal member of many.

Maintaining Your Boundaries

As you engage with multiple groups, maintain clear boundaries:

  • Time boundaries: Set limits on how much time each group gets
  • Value boundaries: Don't compromise core principles for belonging
  • Information boundaries: Not every group needs to know everything about you
  • Energy boundaries: Protect yourself from vampire groups that drain without giving back
  • Exit boundaries: Maintain ability to leave if needed

The Network Effect

Every person you connect with authentically becomes a node in the network. Every group you engage with skillfully becomes a potential ally. Over time, this creates a distributed web of relationships that:

  • Can't be destroyed by attacking any single point
  • Grows stronger as more nodes join
  • Enables coordination without centralized control
  • Creates opportunities you couldn't have imagined at the start

This is how movements grow—not through one massive organization, but through countless small connections that together create unstoppable momentum toward freedom.
