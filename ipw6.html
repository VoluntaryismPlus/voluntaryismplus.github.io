Worksheet Six: Our Proactive Problem-Solving Method

Complete this worksheet especially if you are in a couple.

This is a detailed process for problem-solving.

Here's a succinct yet detailed process for resolving conflicts, rooted in our seminar's philosophy:

This worksheet emphasizes having a "method" for addressing problems before they fester. This structured template helps couples use a productive, collaborative process when a conflict or issue arises, turning a potentially emotional argument into a constructive discussion.

This method is designed to help you and your partner transform potential conflicts into opportunities for deeper understanding and a stronger bond. It encourages early communication, personal responsibility, and a shared commitment to the relationship above all else.

1. Identifying the Issue: Understanding What Happened (Without Blame)

The goal here is for both partners to articulate their individual experience of the situation, focusing on observations and personal feelings rather than accusations. This step ensures that both perspectives are heard and understood before conclusions are drawn.

Process:

Step 1: The Initiator's Perspective ("I" Statements)

Action: The person who identified the issue speaks first. Clearly and calmly describe the specific event or behavior that occurred, focusing solely on what you observed and how it made you feel. Avoid "you always/never" statements (in this section) or assigning blame.

Prompt: "When [specific situation/action] happened, I observed [your partner's specific action] and I felt [your specific emotion] because [the impact on you]."

Example: "When you came home late last night without texting, I observed that you didn't look at me or say anything, and I felt unheard and unimportant, because I had waited up for you."

Step 2: The Listener's Active Understanding

Action: The partner listens without interrupting, then paraphrases what they heard to ensure they truly understood the initiator's perspective. Do not offer justifications or defenses yet.

Prompt: "What I hear you saying is [summarize their perspective]. Is that right? Is there anything else you want me to understand about your experience?"

Example: "What I hear is that when I came in late and didn't acknowledge you, you felt unheard and unimportant because you were waiting. Is that right?"

Step 3: The Listener's Perspective (Also "I" Statements)

Action: Now, the partner who was listening shares their own experience of the same event. Again, focus on observations and personal feelings, not on refuting the other.

Prompt: "From my side, when [specific situation/action] happened, I also observed [your own specific action/thought] and I felt [your specific emotion] because [the impact on you]."

Example: "From my side, when I came in late, I was really stressed about work and just focused on getting to bed, and I felt overwhelmed and distracted, which made me unintentionally shut down."

Step 5: Confirming Mutual Understanding

Action: Both partners verbally confirm that they each feel their individual experience of the situation has been fully heard and understood by the other, even if they don't yet agree on a solution.

Prompt: "Do we both feel fully understood in our individual experiences of this situation?"

2. Taking Responsibility: Identifying Your Own Contribution

This step is critical for shifting from a blame-focused mindset to a solution-oriented one, aligning with the principle that each person takes responsibility for their interpretations and feelings. Each person identifies their own part in the dynamic, however small.

Process:

Step 1: Individual Reflection (Inward Look)

Action: Each person quietly (or internally) reflects on their own actions, reactions, or interpretations that might have contributed to the situation or their own emotional response. This is about self-awareness, not self-blame.

Prompt: "What 'story' did I tell myself about this situation, or what was my own choice or reaction, that I can take personal responsibility for?"

Step 2: Articulating Personal Responsibility

Action: Each partner articulates their own contribution or responsibility, without waiting for the other person to go first, or expecting them to reciprocate. This is an act of putting the relationship first.

Prompt: "My part in this was [specific action, interpretation, or emotional reaction], and I take responsibility for that."

Example (Initiator): "My part in this was letting my anxiety build up instead of texting you earlier to ask when you'd be home, and then withdrawing when you arrived."

Example (Listener): "My part in this was not being mindful of your feelings when I came in, and making an assumption you would understand I was just tired, without communicating it."

3. Finding a Collaborative Solution: Moving Forward Together

This final stage transforms the understood issue and individual responsibilities into a plan for positive change. The focus is on creating a future where both partners' needs are better met and the relationship grows stronger.

Process:

Step 1: Stating Needs and Desires for Resolution

Action: Each partner clearly states what they need or desire from the resolution, focusing on concrete future actions or understandings that would make them feel more secure, valued, or understood.

Prompt: "Moving forward, what I need/would like is [specific future action/change/understanding] to feel [desired emotion/outcome]."

Example (Initiator): "Moving forward, I need to know you're thinking of me when you're late, so I'd really appreciate a quick text if you're delayed, even if it's just 'running late, talk soon,' to feel more secure."

Example (Listener): "Moving forward, I need to remember to prioritize our connection even when I'm stressed. I'd like for us to have a quick check-in when I get home, even if it's just five minutes."

Step 2: Brainstorming Solutions (Together as a Team)

Action: As a couple, brainstorm various ways to meet those stated needs. Remember, the goal is to make the relationship stronger, so consider solutions that benefit both. No idea is too silly at this stage.

Prompt: "Given what we both need, what are all the different ways we could handle this or similar situations differently in the future?"

Step 3: Agreeing on a Concrete Plan

Action: Choose one or two actionable solutions that both partners can genuinely commit to implementing. Make them specific, measurable, and realistic.

Prompt: "We agree that in the future, when [trigger event] happens, [Partner A will do specific action] and [Partner B will do specific action]."

Example: "We agree that in the future, if I'm running more than 15 minutes late, I will send a quick text letting you know. And when I get home, we will take 5 minutes to connect before we disconnect for the night."

Step 4: Confirming Commitment & Future Check-in

Action: Both partners verbally confirm their commitment to the agreed-upon plan. Set a time to check in later (e.g., end of the week, next month) to see how the new strategy is working and if any adjustments are needed.

Prompt: "Are we both genuinely committed to this plan? When shall we check in to see how it's working (e.g., this Sunday)? If we struggle, we'll re-engage this process."
