Step Six: How to Maintain a Good Relationship

Keeping a relationship going strong is a part of the relational process that is often overlooked.

If you follow this course, you have already laid the groundwork for creating a relationship that is secure, loving, and based on mutual respect and acknowledgement of each partner's needs.

But the work is not done yet. You need to make sure that neither partner has beliefs that will eventually undermine your happiness as a couple and create resentment that could lead to the dissolution of your relationship.

How to Maintain a Good Relationship
  • Updating expectations
  • Dispelling the myths of relationships
  • Addressing issues
  • Avoiding stagnation

Myths That Destroy Relationships:

  • The Honeymoon Period

    This is the idea of a period of time at the beginning of a relationship where a couple is more happy because they just met and haven't had many arguments or dissappointments yet.

    In reality, the honeymoon period describes the inevitable downfall of an unhealthy relationship that was initiated too hastily and without the proper process or expectation setting.

    With proper care, the honeymoon phase never needs to end. If you are authentic, you can take constant joy in the process of expressing your true self with someone who supports and loves you.

  • Inevitable Boredom or the "Need for Variety"

    Avoiding stagnation in a relationship is important. If you are not growing or changing in any specific area, your partner might become weary of interacting with your patterns.

    However, the idea that any relationship will become stale without introducing foreign people or situations into the mix is not true.

    If someone is not excited about the prospect of being with just one person, they have not yet met the person who will interest them to no end. They should not be in a relationship until they meet this person.

Change is Good

There is a difference I take to the common rhetoric that “you should not try to change your partner, just accept them as they are”.

I think that most change is good (see my explanation of good and bad here), if only as a stepping stone to a further change down the line. I also assert that if you are a good person, the changes you want to see in your partner will not go against who they are, but will instead enhance who they are and your relationship as a result.

Ask Yourself:

Are there any changes I want my partner to make that would not serve them better and help them become the person they want to be?

After that, ask your partner the same question, and see if you can identify any misunderstandings you have about why they do what they do. They might also learn more about why you believe the changes you want for them would be good for them from your perspective and current understanding.

Addressing Issues

Everything that comes up for either partner is important to discuss. It is very unhealthy for a relationship if one partner does not want to discuss certain issues the other partner has.

Each issue holds within it an opportunity for the couple to grow closer. If not addressed, you become less of a partnership.

Updating Expectations

Troubles in Paradise

So what if the partner turns out not to be the right person for you, or if they were the right one but then they changed?

Make sure to give them the chance to salvage the relationship. This does not mean give a chance to someone who is physically abusive or addicted to a vice that they are lying about. This also does not mean give multiple chances to someone who is not changing. Make a list of things the person can do to get back into a good place with you and be prepared to leave if they cannot or will not follow through.

What if you changed?

If you value the relationship, you should try to change in a way that does not alienate the relationship but instead allows your partner to choose to come along with you. The relationship is not the enemy, it did not create the situation you are in. You created the relationship, and you can adjust it to better suit your needs if your partner is willing to update the contract as well.

This goes back to the initial agreement. You can plan for most things that come up naturally in the course of a person's life as they change and grow, but in the case of lies or hidden parts that come out, that is a breach of the initial contract and trust will need to be re-forged.
